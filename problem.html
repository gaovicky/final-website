Problem

Mental Health


  • The increase in the amount of eye contact people receive on Zoom calls can be stressful. Everyone is looking at each other nonstop. Even if you are not the speaker, you are still being stared at. This is unnatural because people usually look around when they are speaking to someone in person. For example, if you are sitting in a classroom, you might glance at the teacher, take notes, and look at the board.


  • Looking at your reflection can cause you to become more critical of yourself. According to Professor Jeremy Bailenson at Stanford University, this experience is like another person holding a mirror in front of you as you make decisions and give feedback.


  • It’s more exhausting to send and receive nonverbal messages through videoconferencing than face-to-face interactions. For instance, you have to exaggerate a nod or a thumbs up to demonstrate that you are agreeing with someone.

    • Physical Health


  • In-person and audio phone calls allow people to be more active. However, when your day is filled with video calls, you are often stuck in the same seat. It’s not natural for your body to be limited. This sedentary behavior will cause you to forget about good posture, proper hand and arm positions at your keyboard.
